Clinical Vocabulary

Key terms and definitions commonly used in clinical practice, patient care, and hospital settings.

Term Definition
Vital SignsMeasurements of body’s basic functions: temperature, pulse, respiration, and blood pressure.
ICUIntensive Care Unit; specialized care for critically ill patients.
InpatientA patient admitted to the hospital for treatment requiring at least one overnight stay.
OutpatientA patient who receives medical treatment without being admitted to the hospital.
Medical HistoryRecord of past and present health conditions and treatments of a patient.
DiagnosisThe identification of a disease or condition based on clinical evaluation and tests.
PrognosisPrediction of the likely course and outcome of a disease.
MedicationDrug or pharmaceutical prescribed to treat or prevent a disease.
Physical ExamSystematic evaluation of the body by a clinician to check health status.
Lab TestMedical test performed on a sample of blood, urine, or tissue to aid in diagnosis.
AllergyAn abnormal immune response to a substance that is normally harmless.
AnemiaCondition in which the blood has a lower than normal number of red blood cells.
HypertensionHigh blood pressure.
DiabetesChronic disease affecting blood sugar regulation.
CardiologyMedical specialty focused on heart and blood vessels.
NeurologyMedical specialty dealing with disorders of the nervous system.
RadiologyMedical imaging used for diagnosis and treatment.
OncologyStudy and treatment of cancer.
ImmunizationProcess of making a person immune or resistant to an infectious disease.
BiopsyRemoval of tissue for diagnostic examination.
PathologyStudy of disease, including causes, development, and effects on the body.
SurgeryMedical procedure involving operative techniques to treat disease or injury.
AnesthesiaLoss of sensation induced for medical procedures.
Emergency RoomHospital department providing urgent treatment for acute illnesses and injuries.
ConsultationMeeting with a specialist or clinician to discuss diagnosis or treatment.
RehabilitationTherapy and care to help a patient recover after illness or injury.
ElectrocardiogramTest that records the electrical activity of the heart (ECG or EKG).
UltrasoundImaging technique using sound waves to visualize internal organs.
X-rayImaging technique that uses radiation to view bones and internal structures.
CT ScanComputed Tomography scan; detailed cross-sectional images of the body.
MRIMagnetic Resonance Imaging; detailed imaging of soft tissues using magnetic fields.
VaccinationAdministration of a vaccine to protect against disease.
PrescriptionWritten order by a clinician for medication or treatment.
TherapyTreatment to improve physical, mental, or emotional health.
PhysiotherapyRehabilitation using physical methods like exercises and massage.
InfectionInvasion of the body by harmful microorganisms causing illness.
InflammationBody’s response to injury or infection, characterized by redness, heat, swelling, and pain.
HemoglobinProtein in red blood cells that carries oxygen.
PlateletsBlood cells that help in clotting and stopping bleeding.
ICD CodeInternational Classification of Diseases code used for diagnosis and billing.
ThermometerInstrument to measure body temperature.
StethoscopeMedical device to listen to heart, lung, and other body sounds.
AcuteCondition with sudden onset and typically severe symptoms.
ChronicLong-lasting or persistent condition, often slow-developing.
SepsisLife-threatening body response to infection that can cause tissue damage and organ failure.
ShockCritical condition with insufficient blood flow to tissues and organs.
HemorrhageExcessive or uncontrolled bleeding.
EmbolismObstruction of a blood vessel by a clot or foreign material.
ArrhythmiaIrregular heartbeat or abnormal heart rhythm.
DyspneaDifficulty or labored breathing.
CyanosisBluish discoloration of the skin due to lack of oxygen.
EdemaSwelling caused by excess fluid in tissues.
SyncopeTemporary loss of consciousness; fainting.
HypoxiaDeficiency of oxygen in tissues.
HyperglycemiaHigh blood sugar level.
HypoglycemiaLow blood sugar level.
OsteoporosisCondition of weakened bones prone to fracture.
ArthritisInflammation of one or more joints, causing pain and stiffness.
GastroenteritisInflammation of the stomach and intestines, causing vomiting and diarrhea.
HepatitisInflammation of the liver.
Myocardial InfarctionHeart attack; death of heart muscle due to lack of blood supply.
CataractClouding of the lens in the eye, causing vision loss.
GlaucomaEye disease damaging the optic nerve, often caused by high intraocular pressure.
DermatitisInflammation of the skin causing redness, itching, or rash.
SeizureSudden, uncontrolled electrical activity in the brain.
EpilepsyNeurological disorder marked by recurring seizures.
AsthmaChronic lung condition causing airway inflammation and breathing difficulty.
PneumoniaInfection of the lungs causing inflammation and fluid accumulation.
BronchitisInflammation of the bronchial tubes, often causing cough and mucus.
AppendicitisInflammation of the appendix, often requiring surgery.
CholecystitisInflammation of the gallbladder.
Renal FailureKidney’s inability to filter waste from the blood.
HerniaProtrusion of an organ or tissue through the wall of the cavity containing it.
ThrombocytopeniaLow platelet count in the blood, leading to bleeding risk.
LeukemiaCancer of blood-forming tissues, including bone marrow.
LymphomaCancer of the lymphatic system.
HemophiliaGenetic disorder that impairs blood clotting.
MalnutritionCondition caused by insufficient or unbalanced nutrient intake.
DehydrationExcessive loss of body water.
Electrolyte ImbalanceAbnormal levels of minerals like sodium or potassium in the body.
IntubationInsertion of a tube into the airway to assist breathing.
CatheterizationInsertion of a catheter into the body to drain fluids or administer medication.
DefibrillationDelivery of an electric shock to the heart to restore normal rhythm.
ImmunodeficiencyCondition where the immune system’s ability to fight infections is reduced.
